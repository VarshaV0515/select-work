From Inspiration to Purchase: Designing a Self-Serve Tool that Lifted Revenue by 32%.
For millions of aspiring home decorators, Pinterest is the ultimate dream board. Yet, 80% of these users abandon their carefully curated inspirations when it's time to buy furniture, feeling their vision gets lost in translation. At Spacejoy, we saw this gap not as a failure of the user, but as a massive opportunity for innovation.

As the design lead, I spearheaded the product strategy and design for a new self-serve platform. Our goal was to bridge the emotional gap between inspiration and action by creating a "Pinterest + Canva for Home Design" experience. This new tool led to a 32% increase in e-commerce GMV, a 14% rise in conversions, and a 13% decrease in bounce rate.

The Problem: When Inspiration Gets Lost in Translation

Our research confirmed a painful paradox: users would spend weeks collecting pins, only to feel their involvement was "minimized" when working with designers. This frustration led them to abandon their journey and buy items piecemeal, resulting in lost sales for us.

The core user problem was clear: how can we translate a visual mood board into a real, shoppable room without losing the user's personal touch?

Design Process: From User Insight to a Strategic MVP

Our process was grounded in a deep understanding of the user's journey to ensure the final product was both desirable and effective.

Uncovering the Opportunity:

We began with market research,surveys and about 25 generative user interviews to map behaviors and pain points. The key insight was that users didn't just want inspiration; they wanted a clear path from that inspiration to a finished, shoppable room. This understanding formed the foundation of our entire strategy.

Ideation and Validation:

Based on the research, I developed low-fidelity concepts that explored different ways for users to engage with their inspirations. Early feedback from concept testing was invaluable, helping us iterate on our ideas and refine our design. We created a user-friendly interface that facilitated inspiration curation, product identification, and seamless shopping within the tool. Imbibed the insights derived from concept testing into our final product.


Building a Strategic Foundation:

The JTBD framework brought sharp focus to our efforts. We mapped the entire user journey, from "Find Inspiration" to "Purchase Decision," identifying the exact jobs our tool needed to do, such as "Find shoppable products in line with inspirations" and "Visualize how items will look like together". This ensured every feature we built was tied directly to a core user need and an e-commerce goal.

The Solution: A Seamless Pipeline from Vision to Reality

Our solution was a self-serve platform that allowed users to upload their Pinterest inspirations and receive real-time, shoppable furniture recommendations. I worked closely with engineering to design a user experience that made our powerful back-end technology feel simple and intuitive.

How We Translated AI-powered visual models into a User-Centric Experience
Critical Point 1: Users needed to instantly point at furniture in cluttered room photos from Pinterest.
Our Technical Approach:

We used the YOLOv5 object detection model for its real-time speed. It could scan a whole room and place bounding boxes around individual items in a single pass.

My Design Contribution:

I designed the UI to feel instantaneous. When a user connected their Pinterest board, I designed "hotspots" to appear over detected items. This simple interaction masked the complex detection happening in the background, making it feel like magic to the user.

Critical Point 2: The system needed to not just find a "chair," but understand if it was a "modern chair" or an "accent chair" to give relevant recommendations.
Our Technical Approach:

Once an object was cropped by YOLOv5, we used a VGG16 classification model. This model analyzed finer details like texture and style, converting each item into a unique numerical fingerprint.

My Design Contribution:

I designed the recommendation panel where users would see the results. I worked with engineering to ensure that when a user clicked a hotspot, the system would use the item's fingerprint to search our catalog for visually similar products. We then refined the results based on the user's style preferences, ensuring the recommendations were both accurate and personalized.
